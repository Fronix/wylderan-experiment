# Harren's Gate

> [!info] At a Glance
> The nearest thing to a real city for six days in any direction. Stone walls on three sides, a river on the fourth, a garrison that has not been reinforced in fifteen years, and a magistrate who has jurisdiction over the entire northern frontier on paper and approximately none in practice. The old quarter smells of tanneries and smoke. The outer town smells of the same things.

## Origin

The province was drawn at the capital by someone who had never traveled north. The boundary line ran across a river valley where the old road crossed, and a provincial seat had to be placed somewhere. The city grew around the garrison post and the magistrate's house, not because anyone planned a city here but because wherever authority parks itself, trade and services follow.

The gate for which the city is named is the original northern gatehouse in the first stone wall, built four generations ago when the province was formally established. The provincial records have Harren as the first appointed magistrate. Whether Harren was remarkable in any way is not recorded.

## Geography & Layout

Harren's Gate sits on a low hill where the main north-south road crosses a river. Three sides of the old quarter are enclosed by a stone wall, eighteen feet at the tallest point, needing repair in two places near the east gate. The river forms the fourth side. The old northern gatehouse faces the road toward [[Brindleford]] and [[Thornmeet]].

The old quarter is stone: the magistrate's court, the tax registry, the garrison buildings, the main inn row, and the market square. The outer town is timber and plaster, built in the last two generations as the population outgrew the walls. The outer town has no wall and no plans for one.

The river runs along the south wall, bridged near the east end. South of the river, a smaller district of millers and tanners operates on the flat, connected to the city by the bridge. Residents call it the Low Side. The people who live there call it the same thing without the tone.

## People & Culture

The administrative class is small and visible. Scribes, a magistrate, two clerks, a tax assessor, and their households make up the upper tier of the permanent population. They cluster in the old quarter and eat at different inns than the carters.

The larger population is tradespeople: smiths, tanners, weavers, bakers, innkeepers, and the workers who supply and maintain the garrison. The garrison runs sixty to eighty soldiers depending on the season, and they spend their pay in the city, which gives local merchants a settled interest in keeping the garrison funded.

Carters from the frontier know Harren's Gate as a last resort: the place you go when a dispute cannot be resolved locally. The journey is long enough that only serious problems justify it. Locals assume anything arriving from the north has already grown past handling at home.

## Economy

Administrative revenue is small but regular: court fees, tax assessments on registered properties, fines, license certifications. The frontier contributes almost nothing. Frontier settlements have no registered properties and no mechanism for tax collection up there.

Garrison provisioning is the most predictable income stream for local merchants. The garrison buys grain, leather, iron, and wood on annual contract.

The regional grain market runs on a six-day cycle. Grain comes primarily from [[Fenwick]] to the east. Dried fish arrives via [[Thornmeet]] from [[Rackby]] and the Greymere settlements. Timber comes through [[Coldwell]] from [[Stave]]. The market does not see Caltworks salt directly; that trade runs on a separate chain through [[Vess Cordham]] to [[Rackby]]. Factors here have noted the gap. They have not acted on it.

To the south, [[Pellan]] runs a competing market under a more generous merchant charter. The rate difference has been pulling bulk traders south for a decade. Three factors who kept offices in Harren's Gate have relocated. The city cannot compete until its charter is renegotiated, which requires a capital petition that the magistrate has been drafting for two years.

## Tensions

The magistrate's court has theoretical jurisdiction over every settlement in the province: [[Caltworks]], [[Rackby]], [[Thornmeet]], and everything between. In practice the magistrate has never traveled north of [[Brindleford]] and has no enforcement mechanism beyond the garrison, which is underfunded and not equipped for extended frontier operations. Tax collection from frontier settlements is near zero. Collection notices sent to [[Caltworks]] over the past decade have not received formal replies.

The garrison commander, [[Pett Vaske]], wants to extend patrols north to establish a visible presence before the frontier's informal arrangements calcify further. He needs forty more soldiers and a supply line. He has neither. The magistrate, [[Corven Seld]], views the extended patrol plan as premature: asserting physical presence on the frontier creates expectations of protection and dispute resolution the court cannot currently meet.

[[Pellan]] is the quieter problem. The city drains commerce south and the provincial charter prevents Harren's Gate from matching its rates. Seld's renegotiation petition is real but slow, and the capital has not responded in a year.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The magistrate recently received a formal petition from a merchant consortium asking for recognition of a land purchase on the northern frontier. The consortium is the same one [[Bevan Calt]] has been in negotiations with. Corven Seld is interested, not because he knows the details of the Calt claim, but because a formalized land transfer on the frontier would establish a working precedent for provincial jurisdiction reaching that far north. It would also, incidentally, mean acknowledging that every informal arrangement up there, the Calt tithe, the weighmaster's authority, the North Flat breakaway operation, all of it, has been operating without formal provincial standing. Seld has not decided whether to accept the petition. He sent a clerk north two weeks ago to assess the situation on the ground. The clerk is traveling at standard pace and has not yet reached [[Caltworks]].
>
> [[Davan Orrick]] has noted the petition's subject matter. He knows there are no Caltworks filings in the registry. A successful land transfer filing from the consortium would be the first registered claim on the northern salt flats. That makes it a legal priority over any subsequent challenge from informal occupants. Orrick has kept his observations to himself. He is not certain the magistrate understands the full implication.
>
> [[Pett Vaske]] does not know about the petition. If he did, he would read it as grounds for the northern deployment he has been arguing for: formal property rights on the frontier create legitimate grounds for garrison presence. Seld has kept the petition internal for exactly this reason.
>
> The market factors at Factors' Row have noted that no one from the northern salt operations has ever appeared at the market to sell directly. The standing provisioning rate they would offer for bulk salt is roughly what [[Vess Cordham]] collects as his factor's cut at [[Rackby]]. No one from [[Caltworks]] knows this.
