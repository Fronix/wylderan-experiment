# Thornmeet

> [!info] At a Glance
> The first real market town on this side of the lake road, built where Greymere's outflow river meets the south road heading to [[Brindleford]] and [[Harren's Gate]]. It does not produce much. It moves things, charges you for the bridge, and keeps the difference.

## Origin

The crossing was always there: a ford first, then a timber bridge built three generations ago by the Vant family, who held the timber claim and figured a bridge would pay better than boards. They were right. Once the bridge was up, carters stopped fording and started paying. Merchants who arrived with cured fish from [[Rackby]] found buyers already waiting on the south side. The settlement grew around the transaction, not the other way.

Nobody built Thornmeet. It accumulated.

## Geography & Layout

Thornmeet sits where Greymere's outflow river bends south and the lake road crosses it. The terrain is flat and muddy in spring; the road becomes difficult for loaded carts in the worst weeks of the thaw. The bridge is the fixed point everything else references.

The north bank is the older settlement: the Vant toll house, a row of warehouses facing the river, and the market square behind them. The square is open flagged ground with permanent covered stalls on three sides and a clear view to the bridge. It floods at the southeast corner in wet years. The southern stall row is newer and slightly higher than the rest.

The south bank is smaller: two inns, a boat yard, and a cluster of houses where families who service the bridge and the portage yards live.

The bridge itself is wooden, wide enough for two carts to pass, and under near-constant repair. A toll gate sits at the north end.

## People & Culture

Thornmeet is more diverse than the lake settlements. It draws merchants, warehouse workers, toll staff, innkeepers, and the seasonal agents of buying houses from [[Pellan]] and [[Harren's Gate]] who arrive each autumn expecting to purchase cured fish in bulk.

The permanent residents are about 600; the rest are seasonal workers and transient merchants. The town has no formal governance. Major decisions are handled by the Market Committee, a loose group of the eight or ten largest stall-holders, who meet when something needs deciding and avoid meeting when it does not.

The Vant family controls the bridge and the toll schedule. They are not universally liked. They are very aware of this.

Governance has become a live conversation. The settlement has grown past what the Market Committee can manage. There is a running argument about petitioning [[Harren's Gate]] for a formal market charter, as [[Pellan]] has. A charter would formalize taxes, establish enforceable stall rules, and create a recognized authority. It would also give an outside city a formal interest in Thornmeet's affairs. The stall-holders cannot agree on whether this is acceptable.

## Economy

The bridge toll is the town's primary revenue, collected by the Vant family and split with the Market Committee on a formula that dates back two generations and is no longer clearly written down anywhere.

The market square runs on stall fees. Permanent stalls rent annually. Seasonal traders pay daily rates. The autumn market, when [[Rackby]]'s cured fish arrives and the buying agents from [[Pellan]] and [[Harren's Gate]] converge, is by far the most valuable period of the year.

[[Rackby]] fish sellers arrive with their cured stock and sell it here, either directly to buyers or through factors like [[Vess Cordham]]. What the fish sells for in [[Harren's Gate]] is known to Thornmeet's buying-house agents. What the fish sold for at the Rackby dock is known to the boat owners. The distance between those two numbers is not discussed in front of either party.

[[Dellmarsh]]'s smoked eels and eel paste move through permanent stalls year-round. Peat from Dellmarsh passes through the portage yards heading south to buyers who use it for fuel.

## Tensions

The toll is rising. The Vant family has increased rates three times in the past eight years, citing bridge repair costs. The repairs are real. The increases have also widened the family's margin. Merchants who come through regularly notice both things.

Downriver buyers from [[Pellan]] have quietly commissioned a survey of crossing points upstream. If a second bridge goes in two days north on the river, the Thornmeet toll becomes optional for anyone willing to add half a day's travel. The Vant family knows this. They have not said anything publicly about it.

[[Rackby]]'s fish sellers resent paying toll on their own goods at the south gate. The bridge sits between them and their best buyers. There is no other way across. The boat owners' council at Rackby has discussed this twice. Both times it went nowhere because nobody could propose an alternative.

The charter question is splitting the Market Committee. The merchants who have been here longest are resistant; they built their positions under the current informal rules and have little to gain from formal oversight. Newer stall-holders, who have no standing under the current system except what the long-timers allow, tend to favor a charter that would give them enforceable rights.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Corvin Vant is in quiet talks with [[Vess Cordham]] about an exclusive fish-forwarding arrangement. Under the proposed deal, Cordham would commit to routing all Rackby cured fish through Thornmeet's bridge rather than seeking alternative crossings, in exchange for a reduced toll rate that applies only to Cordham-affiliated loads. Independent Rackby fish sellers would continue paying full rate. If this goes through, Cordham controls the lake-to-market chokepoint as well as the salt supply and fish export chain. Rackby's independent boat owners would face both Cordham's factor margin and the full toll on any attempt to sell outside his arrangement.
>
> The Vant family's bridge toll ledgers contain a full record of every commercial load that has crossed the bridge for three generations. Weight, origin, declared contents, toll paid. Sera Vant understands what this data represents. She has not shared it with anyone outside the family. Cross-referenced against Tallin Oss's gate logs at [[The Fork]], these records could reconstruct the entire trade volume of the Greymere corridor for decades, and expose every discrepancy between declared and actual loads.
>
> The upstream crossing survey is real. A merchant house from [[Pellan]] hired a surveyor two months ago. The survey found two viable crossing points, both requiring significant timber work. The cost estimate is high enough that it is not imminent, but it is not theoretical. If the Vant toll rises again, the calculus changes.
>
> The charter question will come to a head within the next year. [[Harren's Gate]] has offered a standard market charter to Thornmeet once before; the Committee declined it. If the Pellan merchant house gets involved in funding a second bridge, they may offer instead, which would give [[Pellan]] a formal stake in Thornmeet's market rather than [[Harren's Gate]]. The two cities are economic rivals. Being chartered under one rather than the other is a real choice with lasting consequences.
