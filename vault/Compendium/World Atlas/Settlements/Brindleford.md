# Brindleford

> [!info] At a Glance
> A river crossing town on the main north-south road, halfway between [[Thornmeet]] and [[Harren's Gate]]. The ford is silting up. The ferry is expensive. The leather workers are the most numerous residents, and the ferry operator's family is the wealthiest. These two facts are increasingly related.

## Origin

The ford here is natural: a shallow gravel shelf where the river runs wide and slow at a bend. Drovers used it for generations before anyone settled. The first permanent residents were a family that charged a flat fee to guide animals through the crossing and repaired carts that broke on the far bank.

The ferry came later, built by the Grunn family three generations ago at a narrow stretch two hundred yards downstream where rock outcrops gave them something to anchor to. The settlement grew in the usual order: an inn because travelers needed to sleep, a smithy because carts always need something, supply stores because the road makes the demand. The leather working began when drovers started selling livestock here rather than driving them further, and the town found itself with a steady supply of hides.

## Geography & Layout

The town sits on the north bank of the river, stretched along the road in both directions from the ford crossing. The ford access and the ferry landing are the two centers of activity; the rest of the town runs between them.

The ford crossing is a wide, graveled approach that has been marked and maintained for as long as the settlement has existed. The ferry runs from a timber dock two hundred yards east, where the river narrows between two rock outcrops. The ferry is a flat-bottomed platform large enough for two carts, run on a cable stretched between the banks.

South bank development is sparse: a few storage sheds, the ferry's south landing, and a handful of houses for workers who cross daily. The main settlement is on the north bank.

The river plain floods in spring. The north bank position protects the town from the worst of it, but the ford approach washes out most years and requires resetting after the water drops.

## People & Culture

Everyone in Brindleford is connected to the traffic passing through. Inn workers, smiths, stable hands, supply store operators. The leather workers are the largest occupational group: they buy hides from drovers selling livestock at the market, cure them in the tanneries on the north bank, and sell finished leather south to [[Harren's Gate]] and north toward [[Thornmeet]].

The retired drovers who settled here are the institutional memory of the road. They know every difficult stretch between [[Harren's Gate]] and [[Thornmeet]], every seasonal ford besides this one, every reason to push through or stop over. They are not important in the town's formal structure. They are important at the inn.

Disputes are resolved in the common room or travel south to [[Harren's Gate]] if they are serious enough. There is no formal council.

## Economy

The ford and ferry are the primary revenue generators. Ford crossing is free to foot traffic and a flat rate per cart and animal. Ferry rates are set by the Grunn family.

The livestock market runs at the south end of town on an irregular schedule tied to drover traffic. Animals sold here stay here. Local buyers include the leather workers, area farmers, and occasionally agents from [[Harren's Gate]] buying for garrison provisioning.

The tanneries are the main productive industry. Hides bought at the livestock market become finished leather. The output goes south. The smell stays.

Road traffic sustains the inn, stabling, and supply stores. Grain from [[Fenwick]] passes through regularly heading north. Salt from the northern trade occasionally passes through heading south, but infrequently and in small quantities.

## Tensions

The ford is silting. The gravel bed shifts each spring and the settled bed is a foot higher than it was ten years ago. In summer the ford still crosses comfortably. In autumn, with water up and silt accumulated, animals that would once have waded through now require the ferry. The drovers pay the difference and resent it.

The leather workers want the ford dredged. Their trade depends on drovers bringing animals through, and animals are more likely to arrive if the ford stays cheap. A formal dredging request to [[Harren's Gate]] has been sitting with the garrison road maintenance office for fourteen months. The garrison is responsible for main road infrastructure but has not sent anyone.

[[Hasse Grunn]] has not formally opposed dredging. He has not supported it either. He raised ferry rates twice in the past three years, citing maintenance costs on the cable and the ferry platform. The leather workers believe the rate increases and the ford silting are connected. They have no proof.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Hasse Grunn has been paying a laborer to dump rubble into the ford approach at the gravel shelf, a few cartloads at a time over three years. The silting is partly natural and would have continued on its own, but Grunn has accelerated it deliberately. Two residents have noticed: Told Wessmark's apprentice and a woman who runs a south bank storage shed. Both have seen a cart at the ford approach on separate occasions after nightfall. Neither has connected the visits to Grunn directly. Neither has spoken to the other about it.
>
> If the ford becomes permanently impassable, Grunn controls the only crossing between [[Thornmeet]] and [[Harren's Gate]]. He has been buying south bank property specifically to prevent a competing crossing from being established. His current holdings cover the three viable landing sites on the south bank within half a mile of the ferry.
>
> There is also an upstream problem Grunn did not engineer. A timber operation a day's travel north along the river has been clearing forest on the eastern hillsides for three years. The land holds less water than it used to. The river runs higher each spring. This makes the ford approach worse faster than Grunn's sabotage alone would, and it makes the ferry cable more expensive to maintain due to stronger spring currents. Grunn knows both facts and considers the first one useful.
>
> The garrison road maintenance office at [[Harren's Gate]] received the dredging petition from Told Wessmark and passed it to [[Pett Vaske]], who passed it to a clerk, who filed it as a non-urgent infrastructure request. No funding has been allocated. In-person pressure on [[Corven Seld]] or [[Pett Vaske]] would likely move it. No one from Brindleford has appeared in person at [[Harren's Gate]] to make the case.
