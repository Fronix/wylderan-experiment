# Dellmarsh

> [!info] At a Glance
> A settlement of eel trappers and peat cutters on the boggy south shore of Greymere. Stilt houses, plank walkways, and families who have worked the same sections of marsh for generations. They know the ecology of this bog better than anyone. They have been cutting past the safe rate for three years.

## Origin

The families came for the peat. Forty years ago timber was scarcer than it is now and the bog south shore offered cheap fuel in volume. The eels were a secondary income that became a primary one when someone realized how well the south-channel smoked eel sold in [[Thornmeet]]. The settlement grew without planning, one family adding a walkway section to the next, until it was permanent by default.

Nobody came here because it was good land. The people who stayed are the people who stopped needing it to be.

## Geography & Layout

The south shore of Greymere is the shallowest and murkiest end of the lake. Reed beds extend deep into the water. Behind the shore the land is bog for most of the year, soft and wet, dry enough at the margins in summer to cut.

Dellmarsh proper is a cluster of buildings on pole foundations connected by a raised plank walkway. The walkways creak. They need a replaced section every two or three years. There is one area of genuinely firm ground, a low ridge of sandy soil a quarter mile south of the shore where the peat drying racks stand and the smokers are built.

Reaching [[Thornmeet]] by land requires a half-day detour around the bog. Most residents use flat-bottom boats along the south shore and up the east bank. The muddy track from Thornmeet exists but is unreliable in spring.

## People & Culture

Dellmarsh operates as a collective. The eel trap lines are communally maintained. The peat cutting runs on a rotation. Decisions that require a spokesperson produce one, usually whoever has dealt most recently with the buyer in question. There is no formal head and no written rules about any of it.

The settlement is insular in the way that small places with specialized knowledge become insular. Residents consider the south-shore channels and the bog margins their territory in a way that has never needed to be formalized, because nobody else wanted them. [[Rackby]]'s expansion along the north shore has produced a low, ongoing resentment: the lake is getting more crowded and the crowding is all coming from one direction.

A younger cohort has been working the Thornmeet stall directly for two years. They have seen market prices. They are not convinced the Rackby arrangement is as fair as their parents treated it.

## Economy

Two products. Two buyers.

Peat goes to [[Rackby]]. Rackby's smokehouses burn through more fuel than the local tree line can supply. Dellmarsh has been filling the gap for eleven years. The arrangement is informal: a Rackby boat arrives at the start of curing season, the load is negotiated on the day, and the price has moved upward most years. Rackby has been slow to pay for the past two deliveries. The most recent payment was three weeks late. The one before it was six weeks late. The collective has not addressed this formally.

Smoked eel and eel paste go to [[Thornmeet]]'s market through a permanent stall managed by a rotating Dellmarsh resident. Eel paste keeps well and sells year-round. Whole smoked eels are seasonal and priced higher. The stall is the settlement's clearest window to what the wider market actually pays.

Salt for eel curing comes from [[Rackby]] at a reduced rate, traded against the peat pricing. This arrangement means neither side has clean numbers about what they're actually paying for what.

## Tensions

Rackby's slow payments are not random. The boat owners' margins tightened last season. They are paying for peat later because they are collecting less earlier. Dellmarsh does not have the full picture, but the pattern is noticeable. If the next payment is late by more than the last, there will be a meeting about it.

The overharvest is the structural problem nobody is naming. Three families are cutting in bog sections that should be resting. The outer margins closest to shore have been worked the longest and are visibly thinner than they were ten years ago. The families cutting the depleted sections know this. They are cutting there because the alternative is longer carries from the deep bog, which slow the work and reduce their rotation share. The collective's rotation system was built when the accessible peat was more evenly distributed. It is not distributing evenly anymore.

The younger residents who have spent time at the Thornmeet stall understand that selling direct at Thornmeet would remove the Rackby middleman from the eel side entirely. Their parents' generation built the Rackby arrangement and considers it stable. The argument has not broken into the open yet, but it is forming.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The peat supply to Rackby is not easily replaceable. The next nearest bog is three days south. Timber supply around Greymere has been thinning for years. If Dellmarsh's output drops, Rackby's smokehouses lose fuel and the entire curing operation is at risk. This is another pressure point in the salt-fish chain, and it is downstream of the overharvest problem that is already in motion. The boat owners at Rackby have not assessed this dependency. Someone who understood the chain could apply significant pressure to Rackby simply by disrupting peat delivery.
>
> One family, the Brent family, found something in the deep marsh while extending a cutting trench six months ago: old foundation stones, worked and fitted, running in a clear deliberate line beneath two feet of peat. They dug around it enough to confirm it is a foundation and not a natural feature. They have not told anyone outside the family. They are not sure what to do with the information and are mildly worried that reporting it would trigger some outside authority claiming interest in the bog. The foundation is from a previous settlement, not anything unusual. It is the existence of prior occupancy they are sitting on, not a mystery. The worry is practical: if someone decides the bog was settled before, what does that do to the collective's claim to be working it?
>
> Wette Hoss suspects the overharvest is worse than she has information to confirm. She has been quietly slowing her own family's cut rate on the near margin to extend its life. The other cutters working the near margin have not noticed because they are focused on their own sections. If the near margin plays out sooner than expected anyway, she will not be able to explain why she said nothing.
>
> The collective has no written record of trap placements, rotation assignments, or maintenance obligations. Everything is verbal and in personal memory. Two of the five families who hold the most extensive trap lines have adult children who left for [[Thornmeet]]. If those lines fall into dispute at the same time, there is nothing documentary to resolve it against.
