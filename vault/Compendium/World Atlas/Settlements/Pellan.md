# Pellan

> [!info] At a Glance
> A walled city on a river confluence, older than [[Harren's Gate]] and still bitter about losing the magistrate's seat. Pellan has the only proper market charter in the region. The market square is the largest open space for two days' walk. The mills run day and night during harvest season.

## Origin

Pellan grew at the point where two rivers meet, and it grew because that point was useful. The confluence made the land wedge between them easy to defend and impossible to flood from a single direction. Mills went up along the faster eastern tributary within a generation of the first permanent settlement. The market charter followed because someone had to decide who collected the road tolls and at what rate, and Pellan's council was the only body organized enough to do it.

For two generations Pellan held the magistrate's seat. The seat moved to [[Harren's Gate]] during a period when Pellan's council was slow to respond to a succession dispute in a northern settlement. Harren's Gate resolved it first. The seat stayed north. Pellan has not forgiven this, and the people here do not pretend otherwise.

## Geography & Layout

The city sits on the wedge of land between two rivers, stone walls on the land sides and riverbanks on the other two. The stonework is old, some of it repurposed from earlier construction whose original purpose no one clearly remembers. The walls have not needed to stop anyone in generations. They are maintained on the gate sides and patchworked on the back quarter where no one looks from the road.

Mills cluster along the faster eastern tributary, six wheels owned by four families. The miller's guild controls pricing and access; the mills run at capacity in autumn and at the guild's discretion in winter. The market square sits at the center of the city, large enough to hold every farmer and trader within a half-day's walk on the same market day.

## People & Culture

Pellan has older money and a civic identity that the frontier settlements lack. The town council meets twice a month and keeps written records. The market warden oversees charter compliance and toll collection. The miller's guild has formal membership requirements. People here think of themselves as the real center of the region, and they mean it as a factual claim, not an aspiration.

The standing comparison to [[Harren's Gate]] is always present. Pellan's people describe the magistrate's seat as something taken rather than earned, and they are not subtle about it. This shows up in how they treat merchants who arrive from Harren's Gate direction, which is with a particular kind of deliberate cordiality that makes everyone aware of the underlying hostility.

## Economy

The market charter grants legal standing to transactions conducted here. Goods sold at Pellan's market are recognized trade on the regional record. Goods sold at informal markets, like [[Thornmeet]], are not. This matters for merchants who need documentation, for disputes that require arbitration, and for any trade that might touch the magistrate's jurisdiction later. The charter draws traders who want their deals to hold up under examination.

Grain from [[Fenwick]] and surrounding farms arrives for milling and onward sale. Wheat and barley flow south through Pellan to river trade routes and east to road buyers. The miller's guild controls flour prices at every step. The charter market rate for grain milling is lower than what [[Harren's Gate]]'s tolls would cost a trader, which Pellan's council maintains deliberately.

The rivers give Pellan access to trade routes that do not depend on road conditions or Harren's Gate's toll posts.

## Tensions

The rivalry with [[Harren's Gate]] is the defining tension of Pellan's civic life. Pellan undercuts Harren's Gate's toll schedule by offering charter rates that leave traders better off selling here. Harren's Gate has a garrison and Pellan does not. Both cities know this, and both cities know what it means if the competition escalates past economic maneuvering.

The miller's guild has been raising flour prices for three seasons. Farmers from [[Fenwick]] and the surrounding bottomlands have no alternative milling arrangement and no leverage against the guild's pricing. The council benefits from guild tax revenue and has not moved against the increases.

The walls are old. The city has not faced a military threat in living memory, but the Harren's Gate garrison is a standing reminder that the relationship between the two cities could become something other than economic rivalry if anyone chose to make it so.

## Notable Locations

| Location | Description |
|---|---|
| The Market Square | Largest open space in the region. Fills on market day, near-empty otherwise. Charter weighing stations at the main entrance. |
| The Charter Hall | Council meeting room and records office. The original market charter is kept here. |
| The Mill Row | Six mills on the eastern tributary, owned by four families under guild agreement. |
| The River Gate | South-facing gate on the land wall, primary entry for road traders from the south and from [[Fenwick]]. |
| The Confluence Dock | Small landing where river boats tie up. Trade from the southern routes arrives here. |
| The Council Chambers | Separate from the Charter Hall. Weekly sessions, written minutes. The most functional governance body in the region. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Pellan's council has been quietly approaching frontier settlement leaders about a charter trade alliance that would route all frontier commerce through Pellan's market charter, bypassing [[Harren's Gate]] entirely. Letters have been sent to [[Tamma Wreich]] at [[Rackby]] and to [[Maren Seedeck]] at [[Caltworks]]. Neither has responded yet.
>
> If the alliance forms, Harren's Gate loses its economic justification and the magistrate may have to relocate back to Pellan or face irrelevance. The council has not recognized that the outside merchant consortium currently moving through the region, the same one negotiating for the Calt claim and the Galden Reach waterfront, would benefit significantly from this arrangement and may be encouraging it through intermediaries.
>
> The charter itself, if closely examined by someone with legal standing, has language that was written for conditions that no longer exist. The council has never tested it against Harren's Gate's magistrate authority directly. They assume it would hold. They have not verified this.
>
> The miller's guild's pricing has been accelerating, not just rising. Held Mirras knows Fenwick farmers are holding grain back due to the poor rain seasons. He is timing a price increase to coincide with spring, when personal grain reserves start running low and farmers have to sell. The council is aware of this and considers it guild business.
