# Rackby

> [!info] At a Glance
> A fish-curing settlement on the silty western shore of Greymere, built around drying racks visible from a mile out. Permanent residents number about 150 and run the boats, racks, and smokehouses. Everyone else arrives in late spring and leaves after the autumn cure. The smell does not leave with them.

## Origin

A curer named Brackham found that Greymere ran thick with perch and whitefish in late summer. He built a single rack, smoked what he could not eat, and sold the surplus to a grain carter passing through. By the following season two other families had built beside him. The racks went up one at a time after that, each built by whoever came for a season and decided the work was steady enough to stay.

Nobody formally named the place. Carters started calling it "the rack settlement by the mere." That shortened. Now it is Rackby on the few documents that require a name, and the rack settlement everywhere else.

## Geography & Layout

Greymere is a shallow freshwater lake three days by loaded cart northeast of [[Caltworks]]. The shore is soft and silty, better for flat-bottomed boats than foot traffic. The eastern rise catches wind off the water all season, which is why the drying racks are there, row after row of wooden frames stretched from the rise down toward the tree line.

The smokehouses sit further back from the racks, six of them in a cluster near the trees, kept at a distance because a rack fire and a smokehouse fire together would end the season and possibly the settlement. The main strip of the town runs between the smokehouse cluster and the landing: work sheds, a few supply stores, the factor's warehouse, and a row of permanent houses pressed close together.

The Bed Floor, a long communal building on the north end of the strip, fills up each spring and empties in late autumn. In winter, Rackby is a quarter of itself.

## People & Culture

Roughly 150 permanent residents own the productive infrastructure: boats, racks, smokehouse shares, and curing vats. Seasonal workers, who swell the population to 550 at peak, do the actual catch, gutting, and packing. The division is clean and not discussed much, because everyone knows which side they are on.

Permanent residents track returning workers carefully. A worker who came back two or three seasons running is considered reliable and gets a bed on the Floor before newcomers do. Reliability is the only social currency that crosses the line between seasonal and permanent. It does not cross all the way.

The boat owners form an informal council, five or six families who handle major decisions by consensus and slow argument. There is no vote, no formal chair, and no written rules. Disputes drag until someone gives ground, usually whoever can least afford to wait.

## Economy

The summer fish run drives everything. Perch, pike, and whitefish are caught from flat-bottomed lake boats, brought to the communal gut tables at the Landing, and handed off to the curing crews. Curing requires salt in bulk: roughly one part salt to three parts fish by weight, packed in barrels, sealed, and stored for the winter trade that follows.

Rackby buys all of its preservation salt through a single merchant factor, [[Vess Cordham]], who procures from [[Caltworks]] and sells to Rackby at a markup. The finished cured fish travels east and inland with Cordham's carters, to winter markets the settlement has never directly dealt with. Rackby sees neither the salt price at origin nor the fish price at destination. It sees Cordham's offered rate, and it takes it, because there is no other arrangement in place.

Two seasons of variable salt quality have raised questions. Some batches cure poorly, fish spoiling faster than expected. The boat owners blame the curing floor. The curers point at the salt. Nobody has a way to check which is right.

## Tensions

Cordham's position is the central problem. He controls both ends of the supply chain and shares information with neither side. The boat owners do not know the real state of salt supply from [[Caltworks]]. Cordham does not tell them when batches are late or short. He adjusts his prices with explanations that are plausible but hard to verify: transport delays, bad road season, higher costs at origin.

The spoilage problem has not yet become a crisis, but it is becoming a pattern. Three families lost close to a quarter of their cured stock last year. If this season produces another bad cure, there will be a formal accounting, and Cordham will be in the room when it happens.

The north shore complicates things. Three fishing families settled the Greymere north end two years ago and have started selling directly to passing traders without going through Cordham's arrangement. They make less per barrel but owe nothing to the factor. The permanent residents in Rackby watch this with more interest than they admit.

Seasonal labor is tightening. Returning workers know their value after two or three seasons and negotiate harder each year. The boat owners quietly discuss whether the curing floor can operate leaner. The workers quietly discuss whether going to the north shore families would pay better. Neither side has done anything yet.

> [!warning]- GM Only
> Vess Cordham knows the Caltworks salt quality is declining. He has not told Rackby. He has already begun quietly negotiating with a secondary salt supplier further west and has not disclosed this to anyone in Rackby. If the boat owners learn he had a backup arranged while they were paying Caltworks prices, the confrontation will be significant.
>
> The spoilage problem is the degrading Caltworks salt. The curers are right. The salt is off. Next season will likely be worse. If Rackby loses another quarter of its stock, the economics of the season go negative for several families. At that point the question of Cordham's role becomes unavoidable.
>
> Tamma Wreich has started thinking about approaching [[Caltworks]] directly. She does not know who to contact there. If she did, the natural contact would be [[Maren Seedeck]] or [[Oran Calt]]. A direct Rackby-Caltworks relationship would cut Cordham out of the salt side entirely. Both settlements might benefit. Cordham would not.
>
> Burl is loyal to Cordham above all else. He knows more about the secondary salt negotiations than anyone in Rackby. He would not volunteer this information under normal circumstances, but he has debts that are starting to attract pressure from outside the settlement.
>
> The north shore families are a stable alternative that the boat owners council has not yet taken seriously. If the Cordham arrangement collapses, the north shore model, direct trade, short chains, no factor, becomes suddenly more attractive. The families there would be willing to absorb more workers but not more oversight.
