# Galden Reach

> [!info] At a Glance
> A coastal port where the estuary west of [[Silthen]] opens into a sheltered bay. The only deep-water docking in the region. Coastal trade vessels stop here. Seven hundred people, most of them organized around the harbor: loading, unloading, ship repair, chandlery, and the cargo coming in from the south that gets carted inland. More worldly than the inland settlements and not particularly modest about it.

## Origin

The bay was used by coastal traders long before anyone built on the shore. The deep water on the north side of the headland let larger vessels anchor where shallower harbors would not. A chandler arrived to sell supplies to ships that had nowhere else to stop. A rope maker followed. A family that did hull repair. The settlement formed around the service trades, not around any founding claim, which is why nobody owns the waterfront in any formal sense. Everyone just got there and built.

The name is geographic. The reach of sheltered water behind the headland gave the port its viability, and the surname of an early chandler family stuck to the first set of buildings. Locals shorten it to the Reach.

## Geography & Layout

The bay sits where the estuary west of [[Silthen]] widens and meets the coast. Rocky headland on the north side, where the water is deepest, a sandy beach running south. The docks are cut into the headland side: three timber piers, a stone quay built out over the water, and a boat haul-out beach for repair work.

The town climbs the headland in terraces. The lowest level, directly behind the quay, holds the chandleries, rope stores, a cooperage, and two fish-salting sheds. The second terrace has the warehouses, the harbormaster's building, and the market square. The upper terraces are residential, mostly families who arrived by ship and stayed. The view from the top looks out across the bay and south along the coast toward cities that most residents have heard about but not visited.

The smell at dock level is salt water and tar. In the warehouses it is smoked rope and resin. The residential terraces are better.

## People & Culture

Sailors, dockworkers, chandlers, and the families of all of the above. A mix of people who have been here two or three generations and people who got off a ship last season and found work before they could afford passage home. Both groups are wary of each other, for different reasons.

The harbormaster position is not elected and not inherited. By convention, whoever commands the most ships currently in port serves as harbormaster. In practice this means the role rotates among three merchant families, the Arvels, the Doss family, and the Clave trading house, depending on whose ships are in harbor on any given month. Each family interprets harbormaster authority slightly differently when they hold it, which is a permanent source of friction and the reason dock workers never know which rules apply this week.

The dock culture is practical. No one who can do the work is turned away on the basis of origin. Nobody is invited to dinner on the basis of work alone. These rules are not stated; they are understood.

## Economy

The port services are the core income: loading and unloading cargo, hull repair, rigging work, chandlery, tar and rope supply, fresh water and provisions for coastal vessels. Ships from the south stop to resupply before heading further up the coast, or to hand off cargo to be carted inland. Goods from inland arrive here and wait for southbound vessels.

[[Silthen]]'s bundled reeds come down the estuary road and are staged at the Reach's upper warehouse before being loaded onto southbound ships. The Reach takes a loading and handling fee. Silthen considers this fee steep. The Reach's position is that the fee covers coordinating storage, manifesting, and dock allocation, which it does, but the fee has not been renegotiated since the arrangement started three years ago.

Ocean fishing supplements but is not the foundation. Deep-water boats run out in the early morning for flatfish and cod. The catch is salted locally. These are not [[Rackby]]'s flat-bottomed lake boats and not Rackby's methods. The fishers here use weighted lines from open keels and work further from shore. The cultures do not overlap.

Goods arriving by ship from southern coastal cities, mostly finished goods, hardware, and worked materials, get carted east and inland. [[Vess Cordham]] has agents at the Reach who arrange some of this, though Cordham himself does not maintain a permanent presence.

## Tensions

A merchant house from further south has been buying waterfront property at the Reach over the past two seasons. They have acquired two of the salt sheds, the cooperage, and a stretch of quay frontage. They have not said publicly what they intend to do with these holdings. The three rotating merchant families are nervous. A harbormaster who cannot control quay allocation because a section of it is privately held is not fully a harbormaster.

The dock workers are underpaid relative to the value of what they move. They see cargo manifests. They can price a load of finished iron against what they earn in a week. This has not yet become organized, but it has become a topic in the lower tavern. The dock workers know they are the thing that makes the port function and that the three merchant families treat that fact as weather, not as leverage.

[[Silthen]] has started asking, through Kel Dusk when he brings carters down with loads, why the Reach's handling fee is what it is and what justifies the rate. This has not yet reached the harbormaster formally. When it does, the harbormaster on duty at that time will be one of the three merchant families, all of whom profit from the current arrangement.

## Notable Locations

| Location | Description |
|---|---|
| The Quay | Main stone-built dock, deep-water berths on the north headland. Allocation controlled by the harbormaster, partly. |
| The Timber Piers | Three older wooden piers south of the quay. Smaller vessels and fishing boats. |
| Haul-Out Beach | Sandy section used for hull repair when boats are pulled from the water. The repair families work here. |
| The Upper Warehouse | Second-terrace building used for staging inland-bound and coastal-bound cargo. [[Silthen]] reed bales are held here before loading. |
| The Harbormaster's Building | Second-terrace office. Different family on the sign every few months. The records inside are continuous even when the name on the door is not. |
| The Lower Tavern | First-terrace gathering place for dockworkers and ships' crews. The upper terrace families have their own. |

> [!warning]- GM Only
> The southern merchant house buying waterfront property at the Reach is connected to the same consortium that [[Bevan Calt]] is negotiating with to purchase the Calt family claim at [[Caltworks]]. The consortium's plan is a full coastal-to-inland supply chain: control the salt source (via the Calt purchase), control the deep-water port (via the Reach property), and direct access to markets in Pellan and Harren's Gate by ship. This bypasses [[Vess Cordham]] entirely, and replaces the informal frontier trade arrangements with something capitalized and organized.
>
> Nobody in the frontier settlements sees the full chain. [[Bevan Calt]] knows he is negotiating with an outside buyer but does not know the buyer also has interests at the Reach. Sura Arvel knows a southern house is buying property but does not know about the Caltworks connection. Cordham suspects something is happening at both ends of the region but cannot get a clear picture. The consortium is deliberately keeping its representatives in each location from talking to each other.
>
> If the Calt sale completes and the consortium secures a majority of the Reach's working waterfront, the trade chain that the frontier settlements depend on changes owners. The new owners will rationalize it: standardized fees, direct contracts, no factors taking a cut at every junction. This is better for the settlements in some ways. It is much worse in others. They will not be asked.
>
> [[Vess Cordham]]'s agents at the Reach have noticed the property purchases but Cordham has not yet connected the pattern to the Caltworks situation. If he does, his response will be fast and probably not visible to the settlements he works with.
>
> The dock workers' dissatisfaction is real and structural. If the consortium takes over the waterfront, they will likely standardize wages downward in the short term, which would push that latent dissatisfaction into the open at the worst moment for the merchant families trying to maintain their position.
